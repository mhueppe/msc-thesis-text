\subsection{Structure Embedding}\label{sec:methods/feature_engineering/structure_embedding}

To represent the structure of a given protein, the \gls{prostT5} model introduced by \citet{Heinzinger2023} was used. \gls{prostT5} is a fine-tuned version of ProtT5-XL-U50 presented by \citep{Elnaggar2021}. It is adapted for the task of bidirectional translation between protein sequence and structure. 

\gls{prostT5} enables direct translation between an amino acid sequence and its corresponding structural representation. In this framework, protein structure is encoded using the 3Di structural alphabet developed by Foldseek, where “i” denotes the current residue position in the protein sequence, which was specifically designed to facilitate efficient and sensitive structural homology search \citep{Kempen2024}. The 3Di alphabet provides a discrete, sequence-like representation of tertiary interaction geometries, allowing structural information to be processed using standard sequence-based language modeling techniques.

Each token in the 3Di sequence represents the local tertiary interaction geometry of a residue with its nearest neighbor in three-dimensional space. The alphabet consists of 20 discrete states that were optimized to maximize structural information content and search sensitivity.

For every residue $i$ in a structure, the nearest spatial neighbor $j$ is identified based on a residue-specific virtual center. The local interaction between residues $i$ and $j$ is then described using a 10-dimensional feature vector comprising angular features, the Euclidean distance between residues, and sequence-distance features. This continuous descriptor vector is subsequently discretized into one of the 20 3Di states using a trained encoder.

As described by \citet{Kempen2024}, the stated in the 3Di alphabet were not manually crafted but learned using a vector-quantized variational autoencoder. The network consists of an encoder and a decoder, with a discrete bottleneck in between representing the latent 3Di space. Each of the 20 characters in the 3Di alphabet corresponds to a learned centroid in this latent space. During discretization, a new descriptor vector is mapped to the nearest centroid, yielding its corresponding 3Di symbol.

Because continuous geometric information is quantized into a finite set of discrete states, the representation is inherently lossy. Nevertheless, the authors demonstrate that structure homology search based on 3Di sequences achieves sensitivity comparable to state-of-the-art structural alignment methods, making it suited well suited for downstream models such as the \gls{prostT5}.

\gls{prostT5} was trained on a non-redundant subset of the AlphaFold Protein Structure Database comprising approximately 17 million protein sequences paired with their predicted structures. The training procedure consists of two stages.

First, similar to its base model ProtT5, \gls{prostT5} is further pre-trained using a span denoising objective applied to both modalities (amino acid sequences and 3Di structural tokens). In this self-supervised task, contiguous spans of tokens are masked and the model is trained to reconstruct them from the surrounding context. This step enables the model to learn statistical patterns in both sequence and structure representations.

Second, the model is fine-tuned for bidirectional translation between sequence and structure. In this supervised training phase, paired examples are used to optimize two tasks: folding and inverse folding. In the folding task, the amino acid sequence serves as input and the corresponding 3Di representation as the target; for inverse folding, the roles are reversed.

For this project the most important part is the embedding generated by the encoder of \gls{prostT5}. The embedding is a structure representative of the protein based on the sequence alone. This is fed to the downstream task of crystallization condition prediction.
