
\section{CRIMS}\label{sec:crims}
The \gls{pdb} does not provide negative data. Meaning for any given entry to the database only the sucessfull crystallization condition is recorded. However, in practice the number of conditions tried is far greater than the ones ending up being sucessfull. Moreover, a model rating potential crystallization conditions would benefit greatly by understanding not only what works for a given protein but also what is likely to not work. For this reason a second complementary data source was used. \gls{embl} is an intergovernmental research organization offering large-scale, high-throughput platforms for protein structure determination, particularly through X-ray crystallography \citep{embl_website}. The \gls{embl} site in Hamburg is closely integrated with synchrotron radiation facilities, enabling automated and high-throughput structural workflows. The beamlines at \gls{embl} are designed to systematically explore crystallization conditions across thousands of chemical cocktails. These experiments typically employ vapor diffusion setups using robotic liquid handling systems loaded with predefined screens. Automated imaging systems monitor crystal growth over time, generating large datasets of experimental outcomes perfectly suited for data-driven optimization \citep{embl_website}. The following describes the data acquisition process as well as the automatic labelling process developed. 

\subsection{Data Acquisition}\label{sec:crims/data_acquisition}
\gls{crims} is a laboratory information system designed to record, track, and analyze crystallization experiments performed on-site at \gls{embl} Hamburg. Unlike large public repositories, \gls{crims} captures local experimental outcomes for a defined set of proteins studied within a specific laboratory environment. However, the data that is recorded at \gls{embl} and published to \gls{crims} is not public. Consequently, the number of proteins represented is smaller as it only covers proteins crystallized from the Itzen work group. However, the data quality and experimental granularity are substantially higher.

A key advantage of \gls{crims} is that it provides both positive and negative crystallization outcomes, which the \gls{pdb} inherently lacks. While the \gls{pdb} contains only successful crystallization conditions—i.e., those that yielded a structure—\gls{crims} logs the full experimental matrix. Crystallization trials are typically conducted in four 96-condition plates, and \gls{crims} records for each condition whether crystals were observed or not. Additionally, the conditions are not free text but rather in a uniform categorical/numerical format. This transforms the dataset into a true gold standard for supervised learning, as it provides clean explicit negative samples for model evaluation.

Across 18 projects, a total of 78 unique protein sequences were investigated. While some sequences were subjected to crystallization trials individually, others were studied exclusively as part of protein complexes. This results in 54 distinct protein entities (including complexes) that were experimentally screened for crystallization. In addition, several sequences represent close variants of one another, often differing by only a few amino acids or even a single point mutation. When clustering sequences at a 90\% similarity threshold, the number of truly distinct protein targets is reduced to approximately 30. 

Importantly, protein sequence information is not included in the \gls{crims} tracking system. Consequently, the sequences associated with all 804 plate–shelf combinations had to be identified manually. Although this process was carried out with care, it represents a known source of error. While the target sequence could be reliably determined for most plates, not all sequence variations could be fully reconstructed.

Each plate contains three possible shelves, which allow variations of the protein solution to be tested. These shelves are typically used to evaluate the same protein under different conditions, such as in the presence of additives (e.g., ATP, ADP, APO, CoA), different ligands, or alternative buffer compositions. They may also contain mutants of the wild-type protein or, in some cases, entirely different proteins. Because shelf-specific protein information is not consistently documented within the \gls{crims} entry pipeline, these details likewise had to be manually retrieved. When accounting for the additions 78 different protein solutions were tried for crystallization. 

This manual reconstruction likely introduces the largest source of uncertainty in the \gls{crims} dataset, as experimenters were often confident about the primary target sequence assigned to a plate, but less certain about the exact variants or modifications tested on individual shelves.

The crystallization drops are systematically monitored over time. Automated imaging systems photograph each drop at multiple intervals. Typically: after one hour, after one to three days, after one to four weeks, up to approximately four months. These time-resolved images enable the use of machine-learning models to classify crystallization outcomes based on visual evidence of crystal formation. Because each condition is linked to the sequence and structural properties of the protein, the system allows us to determine how well a model can discriminate between crystallization conditions that lead to crystal growth and those that do not.

The data used in this study were obtained from the \gls{crims} platform. Although the website does not provide a publicly documented \gls{api}, analysis of the underlying network traffic revealed the internal \gls{api} endpoints used by the web interface. These endpoints were subsequently accessed programmatically to enable efficient and structured data retrieval. A total 645,410 images and their linked condition/time point were retrieved that way.

These images were required to obtain an objective quality assessment of each crystallization condition. To this end, a convolutional neural network developed by \textcite{King2024} was applied to score the probability that a crystal is present in a given drop. \autoref{fig:crystallization_drops_example} shows representative examples of these crystallization drops alongside their predicted scores. As shown, the model exhibits high sensitivity, meaning false negatives are extremely rare. This behavior is desirable in crystallization screening, where failing to detect a true crystal is far more problematic than incorrectly flagging a non-crystal image as positive.
However, this model produces many false positives. Thus, the predictions made by the model cannot be used out of the box. 

Rather, a two-step approach was chosen. Given the high sensitivity the original model was employed to filter out all images that were clearly showing either a clear drop, precipitation, denatured proteins or other non-crystal structures. This removed around 94\% percent of the possible crystallization conditions which represents around 606,400 images. From the remaining 39,000 images 17,000 were manually validated. Given the time constraints not all images were manually validated. Rather, a second model was finetuned on the manually classified images to classify the remaining 22 000 images. The finetuned images presented much better accuracy and predicted 98\% of the manually validated images correctly. 

Following preprocessing, each combination of condition, shelf, and time point was associated with a recorded experimental outcome. For the purpose of this study, only the binary distinction between crystal formation and no crystal formation was considered relevant. Consequently, all non-crystal outcome categories were mapped to 0, while the presence of crystals was encoded as 1.

The specific time point at which crystallization occurred was not considered in this work. Instead, outcomes were aggregated across time using the following rule: if any recorded image within the first two months showed evidence of crystal formation, the corresponding condition was labeled as a successful crystallization condition. Images acquired beyond this time frame were not considered, as prolonged incubation can lead to drop dehydration, increasing the risk of misclassifying conditions as false negatives in the subsequent \gls{ml} model. Conversely, if no crystals were observed in any image within the considered time window, the condition was labeled as unsuccessful.

Another positive aspect of this curated dataset is that little parsing and normalization of the conditions had to be done. Every condition is a combination of canonical chemicals alongside their concentration and if applicable pH. Typos were very rare and manually fixable. 

\subsection{Confirmation of protein crystals}

While good indicators the automated images are not a perfect representative of crystal growth since protein crystals are virtually indistinguishable from salt crystals when using appearance alone. According to \citet{Cumbaa2010}, manual crystal detection exhibits a false negative rate of approximately 20\%, which is critical given the inherently low probability of obtaining protein crystals. To improve validation, \gls{embl} employs \gls{uv} imaging based on intrinsic protein fluorescence. The crystallization drop is illuminated with ultraviolet light at approximately 295 nm, selectively exciting aromatic residues. The strongest signal emits tryptophan, which indole ring contains a conjugated $\pi$-electron system that absorbs photons in the near-\gls{uv} range, promoting an electron from the ground state to an excited electronic state. As the excited electron relaxes back to its energetically favorable ground state, part of the absorbed energy is dissipated non-radiatively, and the remaining energy is emitted as a photon at a longer wavelength (typically 320–350 nm), a process known as fluorescence. Because salt crystals lack aromatic amino acids, they generally do not exhibit this intrinsic fluorescence, enabling discrimination between protein and salt crystals. As seen in \autoref{fig:aminoAcidOccurence} tryptophan is one of the rarest naturally occuring amino acids. Proteins with low tryptophan content may produce weak signals, and certain salts or impurities can occasionally generate background fluorescence \citep{Desbois2013}. Note that false negatives are much more common than false positives. The images provided by the \gls{uv} imaging provide an additional layer of safety when it comes to label quality. This property was used as a secondary label validation step in the data preprocessing pipeline. For each condition initially annotated as crystal-bearing, it was manually verified whether \gls{uv} imaging had been performed and, if available, whether the \gls{uv} signal was consistent with protein fluorescence. If no signal is seen the condition was marked as not-valid.

\subsection{Data Analysis \& Quality assesment}

All of the analysis performed for the \gls{pdb} as applied to the \gls{crims} dataset as well. Since many results overlap the following analysis will focus on the information unique to the \gls{crims} data due to the limited protein space or the extensive chemical space. 
The number of residues for the individual sequences range from 29 (only in complex, otherwise 105) to 975 with a mean of 480 amino acids per protein. Typically the crystallization procedure is multi-dimensional. 

A typical protein crystallization experiment consists of four plates with 96 conditions each, resulting in an expected total of 384 conditions per protein. However, the number of recorded conditions in the dataset deviates substantially from this expectation, in fact none of the proteins report exactly 384 conditions. Several factors contribute to this discrepancy.

For 24 proteins, the most common number of recorded conditions is 392. This deviation can be traced to a \gls{crims} logging issue, where certain wells on a crystallization plate are not properly associated with a condition entry. An example of this can be observed for entries \href{https://htx-hh.embl.org/#/bridge/plates/SD30010842/inspections/5#F5-1}{F5} and \href{https://htx-hh.embl.org/#/bridge/plates/SD30010842/inspections/5#F12-1}{F12} on plate \href{https://htx-hh.embl.org/#/bridge/plates/SD30010842/inspections/5}{SD30010842}
. Similarly, 13 proteins are associated with 478 conditions, which corresponds to experiments performed on five plates instead of four. In other cases, 190 conditions are recorded, typically reflecting optimization trials in which only two plates were screened. Together, these regular experimental variations account for approximately 68\% of the protein-condition pairs in the dataset.

A more substantial source of inflation arises from the data acquisition issue described in \autoref{sec:crims/data_acquisition}. Specifically, some proteins are associated with multiple shelves, but the corresponding shelf variants are not consistently recorded. As a result, identical protein-condition pairs may be duplicated, artificially increasing the number of recorded conditions and introducing false negative entries. The most extreme case is the Lem3-Rab complex, for which 11,113 conditions are attributed. In reality, only 960 unique conditions were screened, corresponding to approximately ten plates. While this still represents an extensive screening effort it is far more plausible than the inflated count of over 11,000 conditions. This error specifically, can be traced back to the screening of multiple complex/ligand combinations without knowing what the ligand variations actually were.

To mitigate this issue, duplicate protein-condition pairs without distinguishable experimental variation were collapsed into a single entry. This approach assumes that crystallization propensity can be modeled without explicitly accounting for additional solution components such as ligands. Since such additions can significantly influence crystallization behavior this assumption is flawed. However, it was adopted to preserve dataset size, as strict deduplication based on full experimental context would have reduced the dataset by approximately 30\%.

\autoref{fig:crims_categorical_analysis} summarizes the categorical analysis of the \gls{crims} dataset.

The left panel shows the distribution of key categorical variables. Only 1.1\% of all conditions resulted in crystals, while most trials produced precipitate or clear drops. The category Other comprises unsuccessful conditions that could not be clearly assigned to either class. This class inbalance has to be accounted for in training either using weighted sampling a weighted loss function.

Approximately 90\% of crystallization conditions contain fewer than four chemical components, indicating relatively low combinatorial complexity — an important consideration for model design. When additives were present, \gls{atp} was most frequent, followed by \gls{adp} and \gls{gdp}. These molecules are concatenated with the condition representation to model their influence on crystallization.

The dataset mainly consists of single-protein experiments or two-sequence complexes, the latter accounting for 33.8\% of all entries. This constrained structure simplifies model design compared to the \gls{pdb}, where complexes may contain many chains. For \gls{crims}, sequences can be processed with separate input heads (one per chain), whereas \gls{pdb} complexes typically require pooled sequence embeddings.

The right panel shows the crystal rate of selected chemicals, defined as the fraction of experiments in which a chemical produced crystals relative to its total usage. Thus, it estimates the conditional probability of crystallization given that chemical. To account for differing sample sizes, 95\% confidence intervals are included, which is particularly important since many chemicals were used only a few hundred times.

Notably, di-ammonium hydrogen phosphate exhibits the highest crystal rate (11.2\%), more than ten times the global average of 1.1\%. While this suggests strong enrichment, the confidence interval reflects the associated statistical uncertainty.

Overall, the analysis provides a concise overview of dataset composition and highlights promising chemicals for future plate design while accounting for uncertainty.
