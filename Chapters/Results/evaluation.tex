\section{Evaluation} \label{sec:results/evaluation}

As presented in \autoref{sec:protein_database} and \autoref{sec:crims} two distinct datasets have prepared. While the \gls{pdb} offers a high variety in proteins the condition variance is comparatively small with a high bias towards certain chemicals (cf. \autoref{sec:data_analysis}). As mentioned before the biggest issue of the \gls{pdb} is the lack of negative data. The \gls{crims} dataset presents the contrary, the protein variance is small while the condition variance and number of negatives is large. The evaluation is thus split in two. First the performance of the contrastive task on the \gls{pdb} is explored in \autoref{sec:results/evaluation/contrastive_performance}, then the model is applied to the \gls{crims} data set in \autoref{sec:results/evaluation/gold_performance}. 

\subsection{Contrastive Performance}\label{sec:results/evaluation/contrastive_performance}
In order to assess both ranking quality and practical applicability the performance of the model was evaluated using the metrics described in \autoref{sec:methods/evaluation_metrics}. Evaluation was performed on both the training and test sets to assess generalization performance.

	\caption[Score Distribution]{(A) Training distribution. (B) Test distribution. 
	The figure shows the distribution of scores given by the model for the actual reported conditions and the negatives in the batch.}
	\label{fig:score_distribution}
\end{figure}

\autoref{fig:score_distribution} shows the distribution of predicted scores for positive and negative protein-condition pairs on both the training and test sets. The scores correspond to the similarity in the learned embedding space, where higher values indicate a higher predicted compatibility.

In the training set, a clear separation between positive and negative samples can be observed. Positive pairs are shifted towards higher scores with a mean of approximately $0.49$, while negative pairs are centered around lower values with a mean of $0.11$. Although there is still some overlap between the distributions, the model is generally able to assign higher scores to valid crystallization conditions. This indicates that the model has successfully learned a discriminative embedding space during training.

In contrast, the test set exhibits a noticeably smaller separation between the two distributions. The mean score of positive samples decreases to approximately $0.26$, while the mean of negative samples increases to around $0.18$. As a result, the overlap between positive and negative scores becomes substantially larger. This suggests that the model's ability to distinguish between valid and invalid conditions is reduced on unseen data, indicating a certain degree of performance degradation and overfitting.

Importantly, the interpretation of these distributions is influenced by the in-batch evaluation strategy. Since negatives are sampled from within the same batch, the difficulty of the task depends strongly on the batch composition. In particular, batches may contain \emph{hard negatives}, i.e., conditions that are chemically very similar to the true positive and may even be viable crystallization conditions. Such hard negatives naturally receive higher scores and therefore increase the overlap between the distributions. Consequently, a certain degree of overlap is expected and does not necessarily indicate poor model performance.

Overall, the figure demonstrates that the model learns a meaningful scoring function, but that generalization to unseen data remains challenging.

To further assess the model's performance, the continuous similarity scores were converted into a binary classification by applying a threshold. The threshold was selected to maximize performance on the training set. \autoref{fig:roc_confusion} summarizes the resulting \gls{roc} curves and confusion matrices for both training and test data. The matrices display the normalized prediction frequencies for the two classes, averaged across.

The \gls{roc} curves illustrate the trade-off between true positive rate and false positive rate across different thresholds. On the training set, the model achieves an AUC of approximately $0.86$, indicating strong separability between positive and negative samples. In contrast, the test set yields a lower \gls{auc} of approximately $0.60$, suggesting a substantially reduced discriminative ability on unseen data. This gap is consistent with the previous score distribution analysis and again indicates limited generalization. The \gls{roc} curve loosely mirrors the recall performance illustrated in \autoref{fig:recallAtTopK} in \autoref{app:results}. Top-$k$ measures whether the correct condition appears within the top $k$ ranked predictions for a given protein. As $k$ increases, the model is allowed to consider more candidates, making it more likely that a correct condition is included, which explains the monotonic increase in recall. 

The confusion matrices provide a complementary view by showing classification performance at the selected threshold. On the training set, the model correctly classifies $75\%$ of positive samples (crystals) and $78\%$ of negative samples (non-crystals), demonstrating a well-balanced performance. However, on the test set, performance degrades notably: only $61\%$ of positive samples and $51\%$ of negative samples are correctly classified. The model exhibits increased confusion between classes, with a significant number of false positives and false negatives.

This degradation can be partially attributed to the threshold selection strategy. Since the threshold is optimized on the training set, it is inherently biased towards that distribution and may not transfer optimally to unseen data. Furthermore, as in the ranking setting, the presence of hard negatives in the batch can lead to ambiguous scores near the decision boundary, increasing misclassification rates.

\subsection{Gold Performance}\label{sec:results/evaluation/gold_performance}

To assess how well the model generalizes to real-world crystallization outcomes, the model was evaluated on the \gls{crims} dataset, which contains true experimentally validated positive and negative conditions.

The distribution of predicted probabilities for the positive and negative classes is shown in \autoref{fig:score_distribution_curves}. The figure mirrors \autoref{fig:score_distribution}, with dashed lines indicating the mean of each distribution. As expected, successful crystallization conditions (positive class) receive higher scores on average than unsuccessful ones (negative class). The difference between the two means is comparable to that observed on the \gls{pdb} dataset under the contrastive learning setup.

When aggregating predictions across all proteins, the separation between the distributions becomes more apparent. The positive class exhibits a pronounced peak around 0.3, whereas the negative class shows a similar but noticeably smaller peak. Overall, the negative distribution is shifted towards lower scores, indicating that the model assigns lower confidence to unsuccessful conditions. 

This shift suggests that the model trained on \gls{pdb} using a contrastive objective successfully generalizes to the \gls{crims} dataset, despite the latter containing true negatives rather than inferred ones. In other words, the learned representation captures meaningful signals related to crystallization success. However, the substantial overlap between the distributions also indicates that false positives remain common, meaning that while the model ranks conditions reasonably well, it is not yet highly discriminative.

\autoref{fig:score_distribution_curves} further presents the \gls{roc} and \gls{pr} curves, including their respective areas under the curve (\gls{auc}). The curves represent the mean performance across all proteins, with shaded regions indicating the standard deviation and thus the variability between proteins.

The \gls{roc} curve lies consistently above the random baseline, achieving an \gls{auc} of 0.664 compared to the random expectation of 0.5. This indicates that the model is able to distinguish between positive and negative conditions better than chance. However, the relatively large variance highlights that performance is not uniform across proteins—some proteins are predicted much more reliably than others.

A similar pattern is observed for the \gls{pr} curve, where variability is even more pronounced. This is expected due to class imbalance and the sensitivity of precision-recall metrics to it. Nevertheless, the model achieves an average performance that is more than twice as high as the random baseline, demonstrating that it provides meaningful ranking information even under challenging conditions.

Overall, these results indicate that the contrastive learning approach transfers effectively to unseen experimental data, capturing relevant structure in the problem. At the same time, the observed overlap in score distributions and the high inter-protein variance suggest that further improvements are needed to achieve robust and consistently strong predictions across all proteins. The improvement of the performance from this dataset to the test dataset for the \gls{pdb} dataset can be explained by the batch dependence of the contrastive learning approach. As the difficulty of differentiating conditions relates to how similar the conditions in the batches are. This means that the ratio of false negatives is realistically much larger for the \gls{pdb} dataset.

Using the threshold derived on the \gls{pdb} dataset, the scoring task can be reformulated as a binary classification problem on the \gls{crims} dataset.

A quantitative summary of model performance across several classification metrics is shown in \autoref{fig:model_performance}. The evaluated metrics include balanced accuracy, macro-averaged F1 score, weighted F1 score, macro precision, macro recall, recall for the positive class, as well as \gls{auroc} and \gls{auprc}. For reference, an empirical random baseline is included.

Overall, the model demonstrates consistent improvements over the random baseline across most metrics. In particular, balanced accuracy, \gls{auroc}, and \gls{auprc} provide the most informative summary of performance, as they are less sensitive to class imbalance. The balanced accuracy of approximately 0.65 and \gls{auroc} of around 0.64 indicate that the model reliably distinguishes between crystallizing and non-crystallizing conditions better than chance.

In contrast, the macro F1 score is lower than the random baseline. This can be attributed to poor precision on the minority (crystal) class, as already suggested by the score distribution in \autoref{fig:score_distribution_curves}. The discrepancy between macro and weighted F1 scores further highlights the strong class imbalance: while the macro F1 treats both classes equally, the weighted F1 is dominated by the negative class, which occurs \roughly 90 times as frequently. The substantially higher weighted F1 therefore indicates that the model performs well on the majority class but struggles to achieve balanced performance across both classes.

Recall for the positive class captures the proportion of true crystallization conditions that are correctly identified. The model achieves a relatively high recall, meaning that it successfully recovers a large fraction of crystal-forming conditions. However, this must be interpreted together with precision-related metrics, as a trivial classifier predicting all samples as positive would achieve perfect recall without being useful. The combination of recall, balanced accuracy, and \gls{auroc} suggests that the model achieves a meaningful trade-off between sensitivity and specificity. This mirrors the finding presented in \autoref{fig:score_distribution_curves}.

The \gls{auprc} provides an additional perspective tailored to imbalanced settings. Since the baseline of the precision-recall curve corresponds to the positive class frequency, the normalized \gls{auprc} shown in \autoref{fig:model_performance} reflects performance relative to this baseline. The model achieves roughly a 2.3-fold improvement over random, indicating that it substantially enriches for true crystal conditions compared to random selection, even though absolute precision remains low.

\autoref{fig:model_performance} also includes the normalized confusion matrix, which provides a more intuitive interpretation of classification behavior. Ideally, all mass would lie on the diagonal, indicating perfect predictions. In this application, however, the costs of errors are asymmetric: missing a true crystal condition is more detrimental than incorrectly selecting a non-crystallizing condition, as the goal is to reduce the experimental search space without discarding promising candidates.

The confusion matrix shows that approximately 71\% of crystal conditions are correctly identified, while about 65\% of non-crystallizing conditions are correctly rejected. This indicates that the model is able to retain most relevant conditions while eliminating a substantial portion of unproductive ones.

To provide a practical interpretation, consider a standard setup of 5 plates with 96 conditions each (480 total conditions). Based on the distribution in \autoref{fig:crims_categorical_analysis}, roughly 5.3 conditions would produce crystals. The model would identify approximately 3.8 of these while discarding around 309 unsuccessful conditions. In comparison, a random baseline that evaluates only half of the conditions would identify about 2.5 crystal conditions while eliminating only 240 unsuccessful ones. Although the absolute gain is modest, the model provides a clear advantage in prioritizing experimental effort.

Overall, these results demonstrate that the model is capable of identifying crystallization conditions and reducing the experimental search space. However, the degradation in performance on validation data and the remaining class imbalance effects indicate that further improvements are necessary to achieve robust and consistently high classification performance.

To further demonstrate the practical applicability of the model in a realistic screening scenario, predictions were simulated on a full crystallization plate. The results are shown in \autoref{fig:plate_predictions}, where the true outcomes are compared with the model predictions. Each cell represents a different condition. Each cell has three shelves attributed with different additions to the protein solution as presented in \autoref{fig:crims_categorical_analysis}. Generally, it can be seen that the model has difficulty distinguishing between the different additives. This is likely due to the limited examples in the \gls{pdb} dataset. 
The visualization shows that the model successfully identifies multiple wells containing crystals while also predicting additional wells as potential crystal candidates. Although some false positives occur, the predicted plate pattern captures a substantial portion of the true crystallization events.

This simulation demonstrates that the model can highlight wells with a higher likelihood of containing crystals, potentially assisting researchers in prioritizing wells for manual inspection during crystallization experiments.
